I started out doing ethnographic research into some of the harder problems in public health — drug misuse, sexual health, road safety. Working with the Central Office of Information, I learned early that the gap between what institutions assume about people and what people actually experience is where all the important work happens. That gap is still where I work today.
Those years gave me something I've never lost: a genuine curiosity about people's lives, and a belief that rigorous insight, applied well, can create real change. We contributed to behaviour change campaigns like Talk to Frank and Change4Life, and led research for the "Don't Let Your Friendship Die on the Road" campaign for Transport for London — work that contributed to an 18% reduction in child road casualties. That taught me what's possible when evidence and empathy work together.
Agency life at 2CV and Davies & McKerr gave me range — global studies for Nokia, Sky, Chanel, PepsiCo, King, Electronic Arts — and a discipline for translating complex human truths into clear strategic direction. In 2012, I also led research for the Government Digital Service on digital exclusion, finding that 18% of citizens were barely or never online. That work shaped thinking on assisted digital services and inclusive design. Accessibility hasn't left my thinking since.
But I wanted to go deeper. I wanted to be inside the thing, not outside advising on it. At HSBC, I joined cross-functional agile squads — UX, UI, SEO, analytics, A/B testing, engineering — optimising product journeys across credit cards, mortgages and investments. No design could ship without proving it moved the needle. I led deep behavioural research into how novice investors experience anxiety and risk — work that changed both the digital experience and how research was perceived inside the organisation.
At WorldFirst (Ant International), the environment was faster and more ambiguous — a major re-platforming programme, limited legacy research in non-Chinese markets, a global multilingual stakeholder base. I adapted quickly: leading rapid feedback loops, driving ICP adoption, taking a mobile app from business case to launch, and helping deliver double-digit gains in user satisfaction. Over time, I built genuine credibility for user-centred design in an organisation that hadn't always prioritised it.
I left to build 12:15. Not because I was done with the work — but because I wanted to bring that depth of experience to the kinds of organisations that rarely get access to it: SMEs and start-ups moving fast, making high-stakes decisions with limited time and budget. The right research at the right moment changes everything. That's what 12:15 exists to provide.
